Wearable Design · Forearm Pod

A 3D-printed clamshell enclosure that hides the ESP32 + 2× AD8232 + battery inside, with panel-mount electrode jacks so no wires hang loose.

1 · Concept

Curved 3D-printed pod (~100 × 55 × 22 mm) strapped to the upper forearm. Electrodes plug in via two side jacks; nothing dangles inside.

2 · What goes inside

Everything sits flat on a 100 × 55 mm baseplate. Battery + charger on the left, ESP32 in the middle, two AD8232 modules stacked on the right. Inter-board jumpers stay short (under ~30 mm) and route around the ESP32, not across it.

3 · External features

All connectors live on the side facing the wrist so cables drop naturally toward the electrodes — they never cross the top of the pod and never catch on sleeves.

4 · Strap & fit

over the pod, not under, so the electrode cables drop straight to skin pads. • If the case rocks, add a 2 mm foam pad to the curved base — it conforms to forearm contour.
Cross-section through the upper third of the forearm. Pod on top, electrode pads underneath, single strap holds it all together.

If you don't have a 3D printer

  • UCL Institute of Making or your departmental makerspace — printers + filament are free for students.
  • Hammond 1551 series ABS project boxes — about £4. Less ergonomic but instantly available; drill the port holes with a step bit.
  • Soft fabric pouch sewn / glued onto a velcro arm sleeve — cheapest, surprisingly comfortable. Less protective.
  • Bambu / Prusa print services — upload an STL, get it next-day, ~£8–15 for this size.
  • JLCPCB 3D-printing service — fine for one-off prints, ~£5 + shipping.

6 · Assembly

  1. Model the case in Fusion 360 / Onshape: 100 × 55 × 22 mm clamshell, base curved to ~50 mm radius. Cut-outs as in §3. Heat-set boss locations at the four corners. Export STL.
  2. Print at 0.2 mm layer height, 30 % infill, supports under the curved base. PETG is more impact-tolerant than PLA but PLA prints crisper. ~3 hours per half.
  3. Install heat-set inserts with a soldering iron at ~220 °C — press straight in, flush to the surface.
  4. Wire the battery loop: LiPo → JST plug → slide switch (in-line on the + wire) → TP4056 B+/B− pads → ESP32 VIN/GND. Solder USB-C of the TP4056 facing the side cutout.
  5. Solder short silicone-wire jumpers between AD8232 boards and ESP32 (see electronics_setup.html for the pinout). Keep each jumper ≤30 mm so the bundle is tidy.
  6. Re-wire each AD8232 OUTPUT/LO/REF to a 3.5 mm panel-mount jack instead of going directly to the electrode cable. Tip = LA, Ring = RA, Sleeve = RL. Lock-nut the jacks through the side wall.
  7. Mount the LEDs through 3.5 mm holes in the lid; solder each with a 220 Ω resistor to one of GPIO 2 (PWR), GPIO 4 (WiFi state), GPIO 16 (leads-off — OR of LO pins).
  8. Foam-line the base with 2 mm EVA — improves comfort, dampens motion artefact, and stops the case rocking against the skin.
  9. Drop the boards into the base, route the wire loom around (not over) the ESP32, then close the lid with four M3 × 8 mm screws.
  10. Thread the velcro straps through the two slot pairs; trim length to fit your forearm.
  11. Test: plug in electrode leads, flip the switch, watch the dashboard appear at http://<pi-ip>:8000/. The PWR LED should be solid, the WiFi LED should stop blinking once connected.
